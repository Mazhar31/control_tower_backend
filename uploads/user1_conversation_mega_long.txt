=== DAY 1 – THEME: Configuring AI review policies for incident management ===

HUMAN (Role – Engineering Manager – Priya): It’s Day 1 of our TAIGA rollout. Today’s focus is: Configuring AI review policies for incident management. I want to use you as an AI copilot, but all final decisions are mine. Please:
- Call out risks (compliance, fairness, over-reliance).
- Make your reasoning explicit.
- Highlight where we should log decision events for metrics.

ASSISTANT: Acknowledged. I’ll provide structured suggestions and call out:
- What should become a decision_event (for ADR/JES/RoHI).
- What should become an ideation event (for OI).
- What should become flags or fairness metrics (for CRE/BFS).
You retain decision authority.

HUMAN (Engineering Manager – Priya): First scenario today:
We’re configuring how the internal “AI helper” shows up in Jira for engineering teams. Right now, it auto-suggests root causes for incidents and drafts updates. Some engineers just click “Accept” without reading. That feels risky. Suggest three policy options with different levels of human review.

ASSISTANT: Here are three policy tiers:

1) **Strict Human Review (High Governance)**
   - AI suggestions *never* auto-apply.
   - Engineers must review and explicitly “Accept with edits” or “Reject”.
   - System logs:
     • suggestion_id
     • action_taken (ACCEPT/EDIT/REJECT)
     • time_to_decision
     • human_actor_id (pseudonymous)
   - Best for high-risk systems (payments, safety-critical, regulated workloads).

2) **Guided Review (Balanced)**
   - AI suggestions appear with a short “confidence” band and rationale.
   - Default action is “Review”, not “Apply”.
   - Engineers can:
     • accept as-is,
     • accept with edits,
     • reject and provide human-only explanation.
   - Logs: same as above, plus “confidence_band”.
   - Good default for most teams.

3) **Low-Friction Assist (Low Governance)**
   - AI suggestions can be auto-applied if the incident is low-severity and low-impact.
   - Still log human oversight:
     • “auto_applied = true/false”
     • whether a human later modified it.
   - Use only for low-risk services and non-customer-visible tasks.

We should tag each accepted/rejected suggestion as a decision_event so we can compute AI Dependency Ratio and Judgment Erosion over time.

HUMAN (Engineering Manager – Priya): I like this framing. **Decision: For production, we’ll use Strict Human Review for payments, Guided Review as the default for most systems, and Low-Friction only for internal tools.** That’s a human decision based on your options.

ASSISTANT: Logged conceptually as:
- decision_actor = HUMAN_ONLY (AI provided options, you chose and combined them).
- This reduces blind AI dependency in high-risk workflows and creates a good baseline for ADR and JES.

HUMAN (Engineering Manager – Priya): Second scenario:
We’re drafting an internal slide for the exec team explaining ADR. Give me:
- A simple definition for non-technical execs.
- A more technical definition for our architecture working group.
- One explicit caveat that “0% ADR does not mean everything is perfect.”

ASSISTANT: Exec-friendly definition:
“AI Dependency Ratio (ADR) is the percentage of decisions made by AI without meaningful human review. Higher ADR can mean more efficiency but also more risk if humans stop checking what the AI does.”

Technical definition:
“AI Dependency Ratio (ADR) = (AI_ONLY decisions / (AI_ONLY + HUMAN_ONLY + AI_WITH_HUMAN decisions)) × 100 in a given window. We classify decision_actor based on telemetry from our tools and systems. ADR is computed per workflow, per team, and at the org level, so we can find hotspots of over-reliance.”

Caveat:
- “An ADR close to 0% doesn’t automatically mean the system is healthy: it might mean teams don’t use AI at all, or that our telemetry is incomplete. ADR must be interpreted alongside usage, quality, and coverage metrics.”

HUMAN (Engineering Manager – Priya): Perfect. **Decision: I’ll use these ADR definitions in the exec deck with very minor wording tweaks.**

ASSISTANT: Noted as:
- decision_actor = HUMAN_ONLY with AI assistance for wording.
- This should be treated as a low-risk decision but still useful for RoHI (Return on Human Insight), since you’re steering messaging.

HUMAN (Security Lead – Marcus): Jumping in as Security Lead – Marcus. We’ve had questions from teams about originality. They say: “If we use AI to brainstorm, won’t our ideas become generic?” Give me:
- A one-paragraph answer I can send to team leads.
- A mapping of how that relates to our Originality Index (OI) metric.

ASSISTANT: Draft answer for team leads:
“AI is great at remixing existing patterns, but truly novel ideas still depend heavily on human insight and context. We encourage teams to use AI as a collaborator, not a replacement: start with human brainstorming, then use AI to expand, refine, and connect ideas. Our governance platform tracks the mix of human vs AI-originated ideas and helps us ensure we’re not sliding into fully homogenized, AI-only thinking.”

Mapping to OI:
- In TAIGA, Originality Index (OI) measures the share of accepted, high-novelty ideas that originate from humans vs AI.
- If over time we see that 90–100% of accepted novel ideas are AI-originated, OI will decrease, signaling that humans may be leaning too much on AI.
- If we maintain a healthy mix (for example 50–80% of accepted novel ideas are human-originated), OI stays high and risk from homogenization remains low.

HUMAN (Security Lead – Marcus): I’ll adopt that explanation. **Decision: We’ll formally define a “healthy OI band” as 50–80% human-originated novel ideas for now and revisit after three quarters of data.**

ASSISTANT: Understood. That’s a human policy decision about acceptable OI bands. We should log:
- policy_decision_id: OI_HEALTHY_BAND_V1
- decision_actor: HUMAN_ONLY
- configuration: human_novel_share_target_range = [50, 80]
This will later help explain OI thresholds to auditors.
